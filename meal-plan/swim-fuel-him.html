🌊 Why swimmers need so much food: Swimming is one of the highest calorie-burning sports because your body is moving continuously against water resistance, using nearly every major muscle group, while also recovering and growing during the teen years. Two hours in the pool can burn over 1,400 calories on its own — on top of everything your body needs just to function and grow.
Estimates based on a 16-year-old male, ~160 lbs, 5'10", using the Mifflin-St Jeor formula for resting metabolism plus activity from school/daily life and training. Actual needs vary by individual — these are a strong starting point, not a strict prescription.

Recommended Meet-Day Intake
—
calories on meet day
🏆 Why this looks different from a practice day: Races themselves are short and burn surprisingly few calories — the real cost comes from hours of warm-up, racing, and the active recovery swims between events, plus simply being at the pool all day. Recovery eating matters just as much as what you eat beforehand.
Assumes roughly 35 minutes of warm-up plus a 15–20 minute easy cool-down swim after each event block. Actual expenditure varies a lot by meet format, number of heats, and how much standing/walking happens between events — treat this as a planning estimate, not an exact number.

⏰ The big idea: Eating something every 2.5–3.5 hours keeps energy steady all day, so practice doesn't feel like it starts on empty. The pre-swim snack and post-swim meal are the two that matter most — they bookend the hardest work your body does all day.

🚫 Foods to avoid right before racing: Skip anything heavy, greasy, or new right before a race — fried chicken, French fries, and large amounts of pizza or cheese take a long time to digest and can sit heavy in the stomach during hard swimming. Same goes for anything carbonated or very high in fiber (like a huge plate of raw broccoli) in the 60–90 minutes before swimming — save those for after the last event.
